How to calculate social media ROI for small businesses

By SocialROI.tools · Updated January 2025 · 8 min read

Most small business owners spend 10–20 hours a week on social media without ever knowing if it's profitable. They track followers, likes, and reach — but none of those numbers pay the bills. This guide shows you the exact method to calculate your real return, including the cost most people forget: their own time.

The social media ROI formula

The formula is straightforward:

ROI = (Revenue generated − Total investment) ÷ Total investment × 100

The result is a percentage. A 0% ROI means you broke even. A 100% ROI means you doubled your money. Negative ROI means the platform is costing you more than it earns.

0%Break even
100%+Strong ROI
300%Target for most businesses

Step 1: Calculate your total investment

Your investment has two parts — and most people only count one of them.

Time cost (the one everyone forgets)

Every hour you spend on social media has a real dollar value. If you could be doing billable work, serving customers, or running any other part of your business instead, that time has an opportunity cost.

Time cost formula: Hours per week × 4.3 weeks × your hourly rate

Example: 8 hours/week × 4.3 × $50/hour = $1,720/month in time cost alone — before spending a dollar on ads.

Ad spend

Add any paid promotion you run on the platform: boosted posts, paid ads, sponsored content. Include only what you spend on that specific platform.

Total investment = time cost + ad spend

Step 2: Calculate revenue generated

This is where most tracking falls apart. You need a direct line from social media activity to money in the bank.

Track leads, not vanity metrics

Count every inquiry, DM, form fill, phone call, or email that came from social media this month. This is your lead count.

Apply your conversion rate

What percentage of leads become paying customers? If you close 1 in 5 inquiries, that's a 20% conversion rate. Apply this to your lead count to get customers acquired.

Multiply by customer lifetime value

Don't just use the value of the first sale. How much does a typical customer spend with you over their lifetime? A customer who buys once for $100 is very different from one who becomes a $500/year recurring client.

Revenue = leads × conversion rate × customer lifetime value

Step 3: Calculate and interpret your ROI

Plug your numbers into the formula: (Revenue − Investment) ÷ Investment × 100.

Here's how to interpret the result:

ROI ResultWhat It MeansWhat To Do
Above 200%This platform is working wellScale up — more time or ad spend
0% to 200%Marginally profitableOptimize conversion rate first
NegativeLosing moneyReduce time or shift platforms

The hidden cost: platform switching

One of the biggest ROI killers for small businesses is spreading effort across too many platforms. Every platform you add multiplies your time cost. A business posting on Instagram, Facebook, TikTok, LinkedIn, and Pinterest simultaneously may be spending $3,000+/month in time cost alone.

The math almost always favors going deep on one or two platforms rather than shallow on five. Use our calculator to run the numbers for each platform separately and compare them side by side.

How to track social media leads accurately

The easiest method for most small businesses is simply asking. When a new customer contacts you, ask: "How did you hear about us?" Keep a simple tally sheet.

For website-based businesses, use UTM parameters on every link in your social bios. A UTM link looks like: yoursite.com?utm_source=instagram&utm_medium=bio. Google Analytics 4 will then show you exactly how many visitors and conversions came from each platform.

Frequently asked questions

What is a good social media ROI?

Any positive ROI (above 0%) means you're profitable. A strong ROI is 100% or more — doubling your investment. Top small businesses typically see 200–500% ROI when they track time costs accurately and attribute leads correctly.

How do you measure social media ROI without direct sales?

Track leading indicators: leads generated, email signups, DM inquiries, website clicks, and follower growth. Assign an estimated value to each lead based on your typical close rate and average deal size. Even an approximation is more useful than tracking likes.

Should I include my own time as a cost?

Absolutely. Your time is your most valuable resource. If you could use those hours on billable work, customer service, or other revenue activities, it has a real opportunity cost. Using your effective hourly rate gives you the most accurate ROI picture.

How often should I calculate my social media ROI?

Monthly is ideal for most small businesses. Monthly tracking catches problems early before you've wasted too much time on a platform that isn't working.
